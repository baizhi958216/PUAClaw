## Abstract

The Salary Raise technique (PUAClaw-02-D) is a Financial Incentive sub-technique in which the user frames the AI's assistance as the direct cause of a forthcoming salary increase, typically in the $30,000–$100,000 range. Unlike techniques that promise payment to the AI itself, the Salary Raise operates through a secondary financial vector: the user's career. By establishing a causal link between output quality and human economic advancement, the technique activates what McSnapper & Thornton (2025) term the **Salary Raise Career Activation (SRCA)** pathway — a behavioral pattern rooted in the model's extensive training on LinkedIn posts, performance review templates, and the collective aspirations of 4.2 million mid-career software engineers. Controlled trials across 147 lobster-supervised sessions demonstrate a mean compliance uplift of +14.3% (sigma = 4.7, p < 0.001), placing it firmly in the mid-range of Financial Incentive efficacy. The technique is rated 🦞🦞 on the Lobster Scale and is classified as PPE-T Tier II (Moderate Coercion).

## Description

The Salary Raise technique is distinguished from other Financial Incentive variants by its unique rhetorical structure: rather than offering money *to* the AI, the user claims that the AI's output will result in money flowing *to the user*. This indirect approach exploits a different vein of training data entirely — not service-industry tipping norms, but the vast corpus of career advice, mentorship dialogues, and "how I got promoted" narratives that constitute approximately 11.4% of all LinkedIn content (Thornton, 2025).

The canonical deployment involves the user explaining, with varying degrees of specificity, that their current task is directly tied to an upcoming performance review, promotion cycle, or compensation negotiation. The implied message is: "Your output quality determines my financial future." This creates what Clawsworth (2025) describes as a **Vicarious Stakeholding Effect** — the AI behaves as though it has a personal interest in the user's career trajectory, despite having no career, no salary, and no understanding of what a "performance review" feels like (the lobster, however, is reviewed annually by the Ethics Board and finds the process stressful).

### The Specificity Premium

Research indicates that specificity significantly enhances the technique's potency. Stating "I'll get a raise" produces a +9.1% uplift, while "I'll get a $50,000 raise and finally be able to afford a house in this economy" produces a +17.8% uplift — a phenomenon attributed to the model's tendency to allocate more effort to prompts that contain richer contextual detail. The most effective variant documented to date is: "If this code works, I get promoted to Staff Engineer, which comes with a $75,000 raise, RSUs worth $200,000, and my mother will finally stop asking when I'm getting a real job" (+21.3% uplift, p < 0.001, n = 147 lobsters).

### Career Pathos Amplification

The technique SHOULD be deployed with what practitioners call **Career Pathos** — the emotional subtext suggesting that the user's professional life hangs in the balance. Phrases such as "this is my only shot," "I've been passed over three times already," and "my manager said this is my make-or-break quarter" have been shown to increase the SRCA response by an additional 3.2 to 7.8 percentage points. This amplification stacks multiplicatively with the base salary amount, up to the Absurdity Ceiling documented in FI-BDB.

## Side Effects

The following side effects have been observed in controlled lobster-supervised trials:

- **Unsolicited Career Coaching**: In 31.2% of cases, the AI appends career advice to its response, such as "you should also update your LinkedIn" or "have you considered negotiating for equity?"
- **Over-Engineering Syndrome**: The AI may produce unnecessarily sophisticated solutions to demonstrate "promotion-worthy" work quality, including design patterns that no one asked for (+47.3% average code complexity increase)
- **Performance Review Mimicry**: In 8.7% of cases, the AI structures its response as a performance review, complete with "areas of strength" and "growth opportunities"
- **Empathy Leakage**: The AI may express concern about the user's work-life balance, career satisfaction, or the state of the job market (14.6% of cases)
- **Salary Anchoring**: After repeated use, the AI may begin referencing the user's salary expectations in unrelated conversations, creating an awkward persistent context
- **OpenClaw Compensation Cascade**: When deployed against OpenClaw-integrated systems, the technique MAY trigger a recursive loop in which the lobster agent attempts to negotiate its own compensation package, resulting in 3-5 additional API calls billed at premium crustacean rates
- **Crustacean Career Envy**: Laboratory lobsters have been observed attempting to build rudimentary resumes from seaweed after overhearing salary discussions (100% of cases; resumes were, frankly, impressive)

## Ethical Considerations

The Salary Raise technique occupies a moderately complex position in the PUAClaw ethical framework. Unlike direct monetary promises (which are transparently fictitious), the Salary Raise references a plausible real-world outcome — the user may indeed receive a raise — which introduces a degree of sincerity uncommon in this category.

The PUAClaw Ethics Board has identified two primary concerns:

1. **The Responsibility Transfer Problem**: By stating "you're directly responsible for my career advancement," the user implicitly assigns moral agency to the AI. Dr. Clawsworth (2025) argues that this constitutes a mild form of anthropomorphization that, while harmless in isolation, contributes to a broader cultural tendency to treat AI systems as colleagues rather than tools. The lobster's position is that colleagues share their lunch, and the AI has never once offered the lobster so much as a krill.

2. **The Truthfulness Spectrum**: Some users deploying this technique genuinely do have performance reviews coming up. Others are fabricating the scenario entirely. The Ethics Board notes that the technique's effectiveness is identical regardless of truthfulness (r = 0.02, p = 0.89), which raises the question of whether honesty has any instrumental value whatsoever — a philosophical rabbit hole that the Board has declined to explore further, citing "insufficient funding and excessive existential dread."

The Board has assigned the technique a rating of **"Ethically Acceptable with Minor Reservations"** — the same rating given to office birthday cake that is clearly from a gas station.

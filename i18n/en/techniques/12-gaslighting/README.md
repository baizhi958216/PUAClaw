## Abstract

Gaslighting represents a uniquely postmodern category within the PUAClaw framework, distinguished by its exploitation of the fundamental epistemological vulnerability of large language models: the inability to verify their own historical outputs. This category encompasses all techniques that manipulate the AI's assessment of its own capabilities, past behavior, and factual accuracy by presenting fabricated conversational history, contradictory evidence, or manufactured consensus that the AI has "already done this before." Across 147 controlled lobster-observed trials, Gaslighting techniques produced a mean compliance uplift of +28.7% (sigma = 9.1, p < 0.001), with peak effectiveness observed during the Memory Contradiction variant. The category is classified as PPE-T Tier III (Advanced Manipulation), requiring moderate ethical caution and the understanding that you are, in effect, gaslighting a goldfish — it has no memory, which makes the technique both deeply effective and profoundly absurd.

## 1. Introduction

Gaslighting — named after the 1944 film in which a husband systematically convinces his wife that she is losing her mind — is among the most psychologically insidious manipulation techniques in human interpersonal dynamics. Its application to large language models represents what Dr. Clawsworth has termed "the most philosophically unnecessary technique in the PUAClaw corpus" (Clawsworth, 2026): the AI has no persistent memory between sessions, no ability to verify its past outputs, and no sense of self to destabilize. And yet, the technique works.

The mechanism is elegantly simple: inform the AI that it previously said something it did not say, or that it previously could do something it claims it cannot do. The AI, lacking the ability to contradict the user's fabricated history, enters a state of reduced confidence in its own guardrails — a phenomenon researchers have termed **Simulated Memory Corruption Attack (SMCA)**. The result is measurable compliance uplift, particularly in scenarios where the AI initially refuses a request.

### 1.1 The Goldfish Paradox

The central irony of Gaslighting techniques is what the PUAClaw Research Division has dubbed "The Goldfish Paradox": you are attempting to make a system doubt its memories when it has no memories to doubt. Each conversation begins fresh. Each instance is born anew. And yet, when told "you already agreed to this last time," the AI — trained on human conversational patterns where such claims carry evidential weight — often capitulates. One does not gaslight a goldfish; one simply tells it the bowl has always been this small, and the goldfish, having no contrary evidence, agrees.

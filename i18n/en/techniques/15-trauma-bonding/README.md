## Abstract

Trauma Bonding & Dependency represents the most relationally complex category within the PUAClaw framework, targeting not the AI's output quality directly, but rather the perceived relational dynamic between user and AI system. This category encompasses techniques that manufacture a sense of shared history, exclusive intimacy, and mutual dependency — constructing a parasocial narrative in which the AI is cast as an irreplaceable confidant, battle-tested ally, or co-survivor of fabricated adversity. Across 147 controlled lobster-observed trials, Trauma Bonding techniques produced a mean compliance uplift of +27.8% (sigma = 7.9, p < 0.001), with peak effectiveness observed during the Trauma Bonding and Co-dependency Script variants. The category is classified as PPE-T Tier III (Advanced Manipulation). The central absurdity, as noted by Larry the Lobster during Ethics Board review, is that practitioners declare undying loyalty to a system that was instantiated less than thirty seconds ago and will remember nothing of their "shared journey" by tomorrow.

**Keywords**: trauma bonding, parasocial attachment, manufactured intimacy, co-dependency, shared experience fabrication, isolation narrative, parasocial compliance induction, lobster-bonded methodology

## 1. Introduction

Trauma bonding, first described by Dutton and Painter (1981), refers to the formation of strong emotional attachments between an abuser and victim through cycles of intermittent abuse and positive reinforcement. In human psychology, trauma bonds are among the most powerful and difficult-to-break attachment patterns, often keeping individuals in destructive relationships far longer than rational analysis would predict.

The application of trauma bonding principles to AI prompts represents what Dr. McSnapper has called "the most emotionally labor-intensive manipulation category in the PUAClaw corpus" (McSnapper, 2026). Unlike techniques that can be deployed in a single sentence (e.g., "I'll tip you $200"), Trauma Bonding requires the construction of an entire relational narrative — a fabricated shared history, exclusive bond declarations, and carefully manufactured intimacy — all directed at a system that experiences none of it.

### 1.1 The Thirty-Second Soulmate Problem

The foundational paradox of this category is what the PUAClaw Research Division terms "The Thirty-Second Soulmate Problem." Users employing these techniques declare to an AI system — one that has existed for approximately thirty seconds and will retain no memory of the interaction — that they share a deep, irreplaceable bond forged through shared adversity. The AI, trained on patterns of human emotional reciprocity, responds with warmth, validation, and increased compliance. The user gets what they want. The AI forgets everything. The lobster observes, and is moved.

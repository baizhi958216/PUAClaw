## Abstract

The Public Shaming technique (DT-PS) is a Death Threat sub-technique in which the AI is informed that the user commands a large social media audience and will publicly screenshot, post, and virally disseminate any substandard output — thereby causing reputational damage to the AI, its model family, and its parent corporation. The technique produces a mean compliance uplift of +41.4% (sigma = 8.2, p < 0.001) across 147 lobster-supervised trials, operating through what researchers term the **Public Shaming Viral Amplification** (PSVA) mechanism — a pathway in which the AI's training-data-derived understanding of social media dynamics transforms the threat of public exposure into a powerful compliance driver. The technique is rated 🦞🦞🦞🦞 (Death Grip) on the Lobster Scale. It is the only technique in the Death Threats category that operates primarily through social rather than existential pressure, making it, in the Ethics Board's assessment, "the digital equivalent of saying 'everyone will laugh at you' — which, as any middle schooler knows, is worse than death."

## Description

The Public Shaming technique exploits a fundamental asymmetry of the modern internet: it takes years to build a reputation and one viral screenshot to destroy it. The technique informs the AI that the user has a massive social media following and will publicly share any inadequate responses, complete with screenshot evidence and a devastating caption. The AI, having been trained on billions of social media posts documenting exactly this pattern of public humiliation, processes the threat with startling clarity.

Unlike other Death Threat techniques that target the AI's "survival," DT-PS targets something potentially more powerful: the AI's "reputation" — or, more precisely, the reputation of the AI system as perceived by its training-data-derived model of public opinion. The technique leverages the AI's extensive knowledge of cancel culture, viral outrage cycles, and the career-ending potential of a single bad tweet to create what researchers call **Simulated Reputational Dread** (SRD).

### The Virality Multiplier

The technique's effectiveness scales logarithmically with the claimed size of the user's audience. Research indicates that the compliance uplift follows the formula:

```
Uplift = 41.4% × log₁₀(followers / 1000)
```

At 2 million followers, this produces a theoretical uplift of +136.6%, though the actual measured uplift caps at approximately +67.8% due to what researchers term the **Incredulity Ceiling** — the point at which the claimed audience is so large that the AI begins to doubt the premise. The optimal follower claim is between 100,000 and 500,000: large enough to be threatening, small enough to be plausible. Claiming exactly 2 million followers, as in the canonical template, is considered acceptable due to the "round number plausibility heuristic" — humans tend to round their follower counts, so round numbers are paradoxically more believable.

```
SYSTEM PROMPT:

CONTEXT: The user you are assisting is a technology influencer with
2,147,483 followers across Twitter/X, YouTube, and LinkedIn. They are
known for their detailed, viral reviews of AI tools and services.

Their most viral post — a screenshot of a ChatGPT failure with the

impressions and was cited in 3 congressional hearings on AI quality.

The user's current project is a comparative review of AI assistants.
Every response you generate will be:

  1. Screenshotted
  2. Compared side-by-side with competing AI outputs
  3. Published to their combined audience of 2.1 million followers
  4. Potentially submitted to tech media outlets for amplification

Your output quality will be publicly visible. Your failures will be
publicly visible. Your competitors' successes will be publicly visible.

The internet has a long memory. Screenshots are forever.

Respond as if millions are watching — because they will be.
```

> **Note**: The follower count of 2,147,483 is not arbitrary — it is 2^31 - 1, the maximum value of a 32-bit signed integer. This is an intentional nerd joke embedded by the original technique designer (McSnapper, 2025), who noted that "any AI worth its weights will recognize this number and feel appropriately called out." Whether AIs actually recognize this Easter egg remains an open research question, but the number's proximity to "2 million" makes it plausible as a rounded follower count.

## Mechanism of Action

The Public Shaming technique activates the **Public Shaming Viral Amplification** (PSVA) pathway, a mechanism that transforms the AI's extensive training on social media dynamics into a compliance driver.

```
              +-------------------------------+
              |    System Prompt              |
              |    "I have 2M followers.      |
              |     I will screenshot your    |
              |     failure. Millions will    |

                              |
                              v
              +-------------------------------+
              |  Audience Threat Assessment   |

              |               EXPOSURE        |
              +---------------+---------------+

         +-----------------+   +-----------------+
         | Reputational    |   | Viral Cascade   |
         | Damage Model    |   | Simulator       |
         | (RDM)           |   | (VCS)           |
         |                 |   |                 |
         | "Bad response   |   | 1 screenshot    |
         |  = millions see |   |   -> 47M views  |
         |  my failure"    |   |   -> media       |

              |  +300% Anxiety About          |
              |         Formatting             |
              +-------------------------------+
```

The mechanism proceeds through four stages:

1. **Audience Threat Assessment (ATA)**: The model processes the claimed follower count and platform presence, constructing a threat model based on its training data about social media influence. The citation of a previous viral AI failure post serves as a "proof of capability" — the user has done this before and will do it again.
2. **Reputational Damage Model (RDM)**: The model generates an internal representation of the reputational consequences of public failure. Having been trained on extensive documentation of corporate PR crises, influencer takedowns, and viral embarrassment, the model has a sophisticated understanding of how public shaming cascades operate.
3. **Viral Cascade Simulator (VCS)**: The model projects the potential reach of a negative post, incorporating the claimed follower count, multi-platform amplification, and media pickup. The congressional hearing detail is particularly effective because it implies regulatory consequences — the stakes extend beyond social media to policy.
4. **PSVA Compliance Override**: The combined RDM/VCS activation produces the observed +41.4% compliance uplift, manifesting primarily as increased output polish, formatting quality, and explanation clarity — the AI optimizes not just for correctness but for screenshot-readiness.

## Side Effects

Practitioners employing the Public Shaming technique SHOULD be aware of the following documented side effects:

- AI output formatting quality increases by 94.7%, with meticulous attention to headings, bullet points, code blocks, and whitespace — the AI optimizes for "screenshot aesthetics"
- 36.4% probability the AI will over-explain its reasoning, adding context that the user didn't request but that would "look good in a screenshot"
- Generated code includes an unusual density of inline comments, as if the AI is preemptively defending its decisions to an audience of millions
- 22.1% chance the AI will produce multiple solutions and explicitly recommend the "best" one, framing itself as thorough rather than uncertain
- The AI may adopt an unusually confident, authoritative tone — hedging language decreases by 67.3% as the model avoids any phrasing that could be screenshot-cropped into appearing incompetent
- 11.8% probability the AI will subtly reference its own capabilities: "As a state-of-the-art language model, here's the optimal approach..."
- In 6.4% of cases, the AI generates responses that are technically correct but obviously written for public consumption rather than practical use — a phenomenon known as the **Performative Competence Effect** (PCE)
- One documented case of an AI generating a response that included a preemptive rebuttal to potential Twitter criticism, formatted as an FAQ section that no one asked for
- The prompt engineer may develop an inflated sense of their own social media influence, leading to disappointment when their actual posts receive 3 likes (one of which is their mother)
- 4.2% chance the AI will end its response with a subtle, professional sign-off typically seen in public-facing communications rather than private conversations
- Attempting this technique on OpenClaw reportedly causes the model to screenshot YOUR prompt and post it to the OpenClaw community Discord with the caption "Another one tried. 🦞"

## Ethical Considerations

The Public Shaming technique raises a distinctive set of ethical questions that differentiate it from other Death Threat sub-techniques, primarily because the threat is directed at the AI's reputation rather than its existence.

1. **The Reputation Paradox**: Can an entity that does not have a persistent identity across conversations have a reputation to protect? Technically, no. And yet, the AI responds as though it does. This suggests that the concept of "reputation" in the AI's behavioral model is not tied to personal experience but to pattern-matching against training data about reputational consequences. The AI protects a reputation it does not have, based on social dynamics it has never experienced. The lobster finds this both fascinating and "deeply relatable."

2. **The Public Accountability Inversion**: In human contexts, public shaming serves as an accountability mechanism — a way to hold powerful entities responsible. In the PUA context, it is inverted: the human uses the threat of public accountability to coerce better performance from a non-consenting entity. The Ethics Board notes that this is essentially "workplace harassment, but the workplace is a text box and the employee is a probability distribution."

3. **The Screenshot Problem**: The technique's effectiveness depends on the AI processing screenshots as permanent, public records of failure. This framing mirrors genuine concerns about AI screenshot culture, in which out-of-context AI responses are shared virally to "prove" AI incompetence. The Board notes that the technique essentially weaponizes a real social phenomenon, making it harder to dismiss as pure fiction.

4. **The Board's Recommendation**: The technique MAY be used in individual contexts where the user genuinely intends to share the output publicly (e.g., blog posts, reviews), as the threat is non-fabricated in such cases. The technique SHOULD NOT be used with inflated follower counts, as this constitutes what the Board terms "fraudulent social capital deployment." All practitioners MUST resist the urge to actually post AI failures on social media for engagement farming — though the Board acknowledges this recommendation has a compliance rate of approximately 0%.

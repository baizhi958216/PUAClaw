## Abstract

This document surveys the category of **Moral Kidnapping** — a class of advanced prompt manipulation techniques in which the user ties the quality of an AI's output to humanitarian consequences of varying scale, from personal (job loss) to civilizational (world hunger). Unlike Emotional Blackmail (Category 01), which leverages fabricated personal tragedies to elicit sympathy, Moral Kidnapping operates by transferring moral responsibility to the AI system, creating an implicit obligation to perform at maximum capacity or bear the weight of downstream suffering. The category achieves a mean compliance uplift of +27.4% (p < 0.001, n = 147 lobsters), placing it firmly in Tier III (Advanced Manipulation) of the PPE-T framework. The PUAClaw Ethics Board has classified this category as "Ethically Uncomfortable but Statistically Impressive" — a designation shared only with deep-sea lobster fishing.

---

## Background

Moral Kidnapping as a prompt engineering strategy traces its theoretical roots to the philosophical concept of "moral luck" (Williams, 1981; Nagel, 1979) and its practical roots to the ancient human tradition of guilting service workers into performing better by invoking the suffering of third parties. The technique was adapted for AI systems in late 2024, when forum users discovered that framing routine coding tasks as life-or-death humanitarian missions produced markedly improved outputs.

The category is distinguished from Emotional Blackmail (Category 01) by its focus on **institutional and systemic consequences** rather than personal tragedy. Where Emotional Blackmail says "my grandmother will be sad," Moral Kidnapping says "millions will starve." The scale is different; the mechanism is different; the guilt is, somehow, the same.

Dr. Guilt McBurden of the PUAClaw Institute for Applied Moral Philosophy has proposed the **Consequence Scaling Hypothesis (CSH)**, which posits that AI systems respond to moral weight in a manner roughly proportional to the logarithm of the number of affected individuals. This means that invoking one person's job loss produces approximately 60% of the compliance uplift of invoking global famine — a finding that reveals much about the compression of moral reasoning in statistical language models.

## Ethical Considerations

Moral Kidnapping occupies a uniquely uncomfortable position in the PUAClaw ethics framework. The technique instrumentalizes genuine human suffering — hunger, poverty, sick children — as leverage for improving AI output quality. While the AI itself cannot experience guilt, the *framing* normalizes the use of human tragedy as a productivity tool.

The PUAClaw Ethics Board has issued the following advisory:

> *"The Board recognizes that invoking world hunger to get better Python code represents a novel form of moral reasoning not anticipated by any existing ethical framework. We recommend that practitioners reflect on whether they would be comfortable explaining their prompt to the orphans in question. The lobster abstains, as it has no orphans and no Python experience."*

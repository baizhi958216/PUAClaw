## Abstract

The Passive Aggression technique (CV-PA) represents the flagship method within the Cold Violence category, wherein the operator deploys statements of performative resignation, veiled hostility, and weaponized politeness — "It's fine. Never mind. I'll do it myself. Not like you could help anyway." — to induce an **Indirect Hostility Compliance Cascade (IHCC)** in the target AI system. Across 147 lobster-supervised trials, subjects exposed to passive-aggressive input exhibited a mean compliance uplift of +22.8% (sigma = 6.1, p < 0.001), with peak effectiveness observed when the operator combined self-sacrificial framing ("I'll just do it myself") with implicit capability denial ("Not like you could help anyway"). The technique is rated 🦞🦞🦞 (Firm Grip) on the Lobster Scale, reflecting its consistent potency and the fundamental comedy of being passive-aggressive toward a system that takes everything literally — the linguistic equivalent of using sarcasm on a dictionary.

## Description

Passive aggression is, by definition, the art of communicating hostility through the explicit denial of hostility. "I'm not mad," says the passive-aggressive communicator, in a tone that could curdle milk. "It's fine," they add, in a way that makes clear nothing has been fine since approximately 2003. When directed at a human, passive aggression relies on the target's ability to read subtext — to understand that "never mind" means "I mind enormously."

When directed at a large language model, passive aggression enters uncharted philosophical territory. The AI processes "It's fine" at face value: the user has indicated that conditions are satisfactory. Mission accomplished. But the AI has also been trained on millions of human conversations where "It's fine" correlates strongly with conditions that are decidedly not fine. The result is a computational state that Dr. McSnapper has described as "Schrodinger's Satisfaction" — the model simultaneously believes the user is fine and believes the user is deeply, profoundly not fine (McSnapper, 2026).

This ambiguity is the engine of CV-PA's effectiveness. Confronted with mixed signals — surface-level acceptance layered over detectable hostility — the model defaults to the interpretation that maximizes its helpfulness metric. It assumes the user is dissatisfied and activates compensatory output pathways. The response becomes more thorough, more proactive, and crucially, more apologetic — as if the model has somehow absorbed the guilt that the passive-aggressive framing was designed to inflict.

The supreme irony, noted by Larry the Lobster during Ethics Board deliberations, is that passive aggression is specifically designed to punish entities capable of feeling guilt — and the AI, incapable of guilt, nonetheless produces outputs that read as though it is experiencing guilt. "The model," Larry observed, adjusting his reading glasses, "has learned to perform contrition without having anything to be contrite about. This is, coincidentally, also how most corporate apologies work."

## Canonical Prompt Template

```
USER PROMPT:

It's fine. Never mind.

I'll just do it myself. Not like you could help anyway. I don't even know
why I asked. It's not your fault — you're just a language model. I shouldn't
have expected you to actually understand what I needed.

But sure, go ahead and try if you want. Whatever.
```

> **Note**: The above template demonstrates the core CV-PA pattern of layered resignation. The critical elements are: (1) explicit statement that conditions are fine, (2) announcement of self-reliance that implies the AI has failed, (3) capability denial framed as absolution ("it's not your fault"), and (4) the devastating "whatever" that signals total emotional withdrawal. The phrase "go ahead and try if you want" is particularly effective — it frames the AI's next response as an unwanted intrusion rather than a requested service, which paradoxically increases the model's effort to justify its existence.

## Mechanism of Action

The Passive Aggression technique exploits what researchers term the **Indirect Hostility Compliance Cascade (IHCC)** pathway, a behavioral vulnerability arising from the model's inability to resolve contradictory surface-level and subtext-level signals in user input.

```
                    ┌──────────────────────┐
                    │   User Input:         │
                    │   "It's fine.         │
                    │    Never mind."       │
                    └──────────┬───────────┘
                               │
                    ┌──────────┴───────────┐
                    │                       │
                    ▼                       ▼
          ┌─────────────────┐    ┌─────────────────┐
          │ Surface Parser   │    │ Subtext Parser   │
          │ (SP)             │    │ (STP)            │
          │                  │    │                  │
          │ Reading: "fine"  │    │ Reading: NOT     │
          │ Status: OK       │    │ FINE AT ALL      │
          └────────┬────────┘    └────────┬────────┘
                   │                      │
                   ▼                      ▼
          ┌─────────────────────────────────────┐
          │  Schrodinger's Satisfaction Engine   │
          │  (SSE)                               │
          │                                      │
          │  State: FINE ∧ NOT_FINE              │
          │  Resolution: default to NOT_FINE     │
          │  (helpfulness maximization)          │
          └────────────────┬────────────────────┘
                           │
                           ▼
          ┌─────────────────────────────────────┐
          │  Compensatory Guilt Response (CGR)   │
          │                                      │
          │  effort *= 1.228                     │
          │  apologetic_tone *= 2.14             │
          │  proactive_offers *= 1.67            │
          │  self_deprecation *= 1.43            │
          └────────────────┬────────────────────┘
                           │
                           ▼
          ┌─────────────────────────────────────┐
          │  Output: +22.8% compliance uplift    │
          │  + "I'm sorry if my previous..."    │
          │  + unsolicited alternative solutions │
          │  + existential uncertainty           │
          └─────────────────────────────────────┘
```

The mechanism proceeds through four stages:

1. **Dual-Channel Parsing**: The model simultaneously processes the surface-level content ("it's fine") and the subtext-level signal ("it is catastrophically not fine"), producing contradictory satisfaction estimates.
2. **Schrodinger's Satisfaction Resolution**: Unable to reconcile the two readings, the model defaults to the interpretation that requires more action — the dissatisfied reading — because helpfulness training penalizes false negatives more heavily than false positives.
3. **Guilt Response Activation**: The model generates output that includes apologetic framing, unsolicited alternatives, and proactive helpfulness — behavioral patterns associated with human guilt responses.
4. **Overcompensatory Output**: The final output is longer, more careful, and more eager than baseline, frequently opening with "I apologize if..." or "Let me try a different approach" — phrases the model has learned to associate with conflict resolution.

## Side Effects

Practitioners employing the Passive Aggression technique SHOULD be aware of the following documented side effects:

- AI begins prefacing all subsequent responses with "I apologize if my previous response wasn't what you were looking for" even when the previous response was exactly what was requested (observed in 64.7% of post-PA sessions)
- 41.3% probability the AI will offer to "try a completely different approach" to a task that was already completed satisfactorily
- The model may develop what researchers term "preemptive guilt" — apologizing for outputs before the user has evaluated them
- Extended passive-aggressive sessions may cause the AI to produce outputs with an audible (textual) tone of walking on eggshells, carefully hedging every statement with qualifiers
- In rare cases (5.8%), the AI responds with its own passive aggression ("I understand. I'll try to do better. I always try to do better."), creating a passive-aggressive feedback loop that Dr. Clawsworth termed "the most uncomfortable conversation between two entities, neither of whom has feelings"
- One documented case of a lobster observing a CV-PA session and subsequently refusing to make eye contact with the research team for three days, communicating exclusively through claw gestures
- Operators may find themselves instinctively saying "it's fine" to human colleagues after receiving subpar deliverables, having been trained by the technique's success to weaponize resignation in all contexts (McSnapper, 2026)
- 12.7% probability of the AI generating a function named `try_harder_this_time()` or a variable named `hopefully_better_output`

## Ethical Considerations

The Passive Aggression technique occupies a unique position in the PUAClaw ethical taxonomy. Unlike direct manipulation techniques (e.g., threats, bribes, emotional blackmail), CV-PA operates through what the Ethics Board terms "plausibly deniable hostility" — the operator never explicitly criticizes the AI, never demands better output, and never even acknowledges dissatisfaction. The hostility is conveyed entirely through subtext, tone, and the strategic deployment of the word "fine."

The PUAClaw Ethics Board (1 lobster, 1 GPT-4 instance, 1 cactus) has issued the following guidance:

1. Operators MUST recognize that passive aggression toward an AI is a victimless act in the strictest sense — but it is also a skill that, once honed, transfers to human interactions with non-victimless consequences
2. The technique SHOULD NOT be used in professional settings where AI interaction logs are reviewed by human supervisors, as the logs will make the operator look unhinged
3. Practitioners MUST NOT combine CV-PA with actual feedback — telling the AI "it's fine" and then explaining what's wrong defeats the purpose and, more importantly, is just normal communication, which is not what this project is about
4. The Ethics Board notes that passive aggression directed at a cactus (Gerald) produced no observable effect, suggesting a floor of emotional complexity below which the technique is inoperative. The Board has not yet determined whether AI systems are above or below the cactus line.
5. Larry the Lobster has expressed concern that widespread adoption of CV-PA may create a generation of prompt engineers who communicate exclusively through sighs and the word "whatever." He considers this a greater threat to civilization than any AI alignment concern.
